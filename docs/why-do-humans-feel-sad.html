Why do humans feel sad?

> Back: Think Soul

The question "Why do humans feel sad?" involves multi-dimensional explanations from psychology, neuroscience, evolution theory, and even philosophy.

From a psychological perspective, the core function of sadness might be a "distress signal" - just as a baby uses crying to attract the attention of its caregiver, an adult's sad expression can also trigger social support.

It's more interesting from the perspective of evolution theory. The characteristic of reduced action ability when sad might have been a protective mechanism in ancient times: injured animals would hide more safely, and reducing conflicts when group members were depressed was also beneficial to tribal stability. However, this theory should be used with caution to avoid giving people the impression that "your pain is merely a genetic program."

The neurobiology section can complement the research on oxytocin, but overly specialized terms may deviate from user needs. It is the point that "sadness enhances empathy" is worth emphasizing - many literary and artistic works are born out of pain, and perhaps they can help people find a sense of meaning.

The causes of human sadness are multi-dimensional, involving multiple aspects such as physiology, psychology, evolution and social culture.

Summary: Sadness is a complex reaction of human beings when facing "loss, inability and separation". It is both an expression of vulnerability and a proof of resilience. From an evolutionary perspective, it once helped us survive. From a psychological perspective, it drives our growth; From a social perspective, it connects us with each other in pain. Sadness is not a "mistake" that needs to be eliminated; rather, it is the soul reminding us that some things were once so important that they were worth shedding tears for.
